A support intake or service consultation can feel easier when families know what information to bring. You do not need to have every answer ready, but a little preparation can make the first conversation more useful.

The goal of intake is to understand the person, the setting, the support need, and whether the service may be a fit.

## Start with the support need

Before the consultation, write down what prompted you to reach out. Try to describe the need in practical terms.

Examples may include:

- daytime structure is needed
- caregivers need respite
- home routines are difficult
- community outings need support
- 1:1 staffing is needed
- a care setting needs coverage
- transitions or safety concerns are increasing

Clear examples help the provider understand what is happening day to day.

## Gather basic information

Helpful information may include:

- age of the person needing support
- diagnosis or known support needs
- communication style
- safety considerations
- current routines
- preferred days and times
- service region
- funding or private-pay planning questions

Families do not need to share everything at once, but these details help guide the conversation.

## Think about goals

Support goals do not need to be complicated. They may include:

- more routine
- safer outings
- caregiver relief
- improved participation
- life-skills practice
- better communication around needs
- more dependable coverage

The clearer the goals, the easier it is to discuss service fit.

## Prepare questions

Families may want to ask:

- What services are available in our region?
- What information is needed for review?
- What schedule options may be possible?
- What does the next step look like?
- Are there limits to what can be provided?
- What happens if support needs change?

Good questions help families avoid confusion later.

## Be honest about challenges

Providers can plan better when families are clear about what is difficult. This may include behaviour concerns, personal care needs, supervision needs, transportation needs, or past support experiences.

Being honest does not make a family harder to support. It helps the provider understand whether the service can be safe, practical, and appropriate.
