Special Services at Home, often called SSAH, is an Ontario program for families caring for a child with a physical and/or developmental disability. It can help families purchase supports that make daily life, growth, development, and caregiver relief more manageable.

This article is for general education only. Families should always check current Ontario program guidance and confirm eligible expenses before making decisions.

## Who SSAH is for

SSAH is for children who need extra support beyond what is expected for their age because of a physical and/or developmental disability. The support is meant to help the child participate in daily activities at home and in the community.

Families often explore SSAH when they need help with:

- routines at home
- community participation
- safety and supervision needs
- respite for parents or caregivers
- activities that support growth and development
- practical support that complements other services

## What SSAH may help with

Ontario describes SSAH as support for three broad areas: day-to-day activities, growth and development, and caregiver support such as respite.

Depending on current program rules, families may think about support connected to:

- daily routines
- self-care and independence
- sensory needs
- safety in everyday activities
- learning or tutoring support
- social skills and community participation
- respite for parents and main caregivers

The key question is whether the expense fits the program purpose and current eligible expense rules.

## What SSAH is not meant to cover

SSAH is not designed to cover every family expense. Ontario lists some costs as ineligible, including many basic living expenses, certain home-related costs, some services funded through other government programs, and supports provided outside Ontario unless specific approval rules apply.

If an expense is unclear, avoid guessing. Check the official guidelines or contact the appropriate ministry office before spending.

## How to prepare before requesting support

Families can make the process easier by writing down:

- the child's diagnosis or support needs
- where support is needed most
- daily routines that are difficult to manage
- caregiver relief needs
- safety or supervision concerns
- goals for community participation or skill-building
- preferred schedule, region, and type of support

This information also helps when contacting a service provider, because it makes the support request clearer from the start.

## Where Golden Care may fit

Golden Care provides [Home & Community Staffing](/community-home-support) for children, youth, and adults with complex needs. Families may inquire about respite, life-skills support, community participation, and 1:1 support where service area and support fit align.

Submitting an inquiry does not guarantee acceptance, staffing availability, or funding approval. It does help start a clearer conversation about what support is needed.

## Plan early for transitions

SSAH is a children's program. Families should begin planning before a young person turns 18, because adult supports may involve different systems such as Developmental Services Ontario and Passport funding.
