Caregiving can be meaningful and exhausting at the same time. Families supporting a child, youth, or adult with complex needs often carry constant responsibility, even when things look calm from the outside.

Respite support gives caregivers planned breathing room while helping the person receiving support stay safe, engaged, and connected to routine.

## Burnout can build slowly

Caregiver burnout does not always happen suddenly. It can build through:

- interrupted sleep
- constant supervision
- frequent appointments
- behaviour or safety concerns
- limited time for work or errands
- reduced time with other family members
- feeling like there is no backup plan

Families may not notice how much pressure they are carrying until they are already depleted.

## What respite support can do

Respite support can help by creating predictable relief. Depending on the family, this may mean:

- a few hours of support at home
- support during outings or activities
- help during difficult parts of the day
- weekend or evening coverage where available
- support that allows caregivers to rest, work, or plan

The goal is not just time away. The goal is sustainable family life.

## Respite works best with structure

Good respite is not random. It should be planned around the person's needs and the family's real schedule.

Families can prepare by identifying:

- the best time of day for support
- routines that need the most help
- safety or communication needs
- calming strategies that work
- preferred activities
- what the caregiver needs time for

Clear planning helps support feel smoother for everyone.

## Respite can support the whole household

When caregivers have relief, the whole household can benefit. Parents and guardians may have more capacity for work, siblings, appointments, rest, or simply being present without constant pressure.

The person receiving support also benefits when routines are calmer and caregivers have more energy.

## When to ask for help

Families do not need to wait until they are in crisis. If daily life is becoming harder to manage, planned respite may be a practical next step.
